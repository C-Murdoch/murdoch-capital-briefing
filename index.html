Tuesday 28 April 2026

1. Global Macro

  • The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) began its two-day meeting today, with a policy decision due Wednesday 29 April at 2:00 pm ET. Markets widely expect the Fed to hold rates steady as spiking energy prices complicate the inflation outlook, while Chair Powell's tenure ends on 15 May; Kevin Warsh has been nominated as his successor.
  • US CPI for March printed at +3.3% year-on-year (all items) and +2.6% core (ex-food and energy), up 0.9% month-on-month - the strongest monthly gain in several months, largely reflecting the oil shock. April CPI is not due until 12 May; the PCE release follows on 30 April.
  • The ECB held its three key rates unchanged at its February meeting, with the Governing Council reaffirming that inflation should stabilise at the 2% target in the medium term. The next scheduled ECB decision is in June.
  • The RBA has its rate decision meeting on 30 April - no change is widely anticipated given elevated global inflation pressures and the currency's recent softness.
  • Geopolitical risk remains the dominant macro variable: the IEA has characterised the Iran conflict, now in its ninth week, as the largest energy supply shock on record, materially lifting global inflation expectations and complicating the rate-cut path for major central banks.

2. Equity Markets

  • The S&P 500 closed at a fresh all-time high of 7,173.91 on Monday (+0.12%), and the Nasdaq gained +0.20%, both supported by continued strength in large-cap technology. However, the Dow Jones fell 0.13% and the equal-weight S&P 500 declined 0.12%, indicating narrow market breadth.
  • Nvidia surged +4.0% on Monday, reaching a second consecutive all-time high and now up approximately 31% since 30 March - reinforcing the bifurcation between mega-cap AI leaders and the broader market.
  • ASX 200 futures were pointing to a weak open of around -61 pts (-0.69%) as of 8:30 am AEST, extending a run of losses. The index is on track for its sixth consecutive down-day and is hovering near a two-week low.
  • Sector weakness on Wall Street was concentrated in Staples, Real Estate, Discretionary, Healthcare and Materials - consistent with stagflationary risk pricing as elevated oil feeds into cost pressures.
  • Markets are in a holding pattern ahead of tomorrow's FOMC statement and Chair Powell's final scheduled press conference, with the Fed's language on inflation and the policy path the key catalyst for direction.

4. Commodities & FX

  • Brent crude oil surged to approximately $108/bbl on Monday (+2.75%) before settling back to around $105/bbl today; WTI is trading near $96.65/bbl (+2.35%) - both elevated significantly by the ongoing Iran-related supply shock.
  • Gold is trading at approximately $4,683-4,689/oz, as safe-haven demand from geopolitical risk competes with headwinds from higher-for-longer rate expectations; gold remains well above its early-2026 levels.
  • The AUD/USD fell to 0.7180, down 0.10% on the session, weighed by risk-off sentiment, a firmer USD, and concerns that higher oil prices will compress Australian trade terms via their impact on global growth.
  • Iron ore is trading at approximately $105.85/t, near a multi-month low last seen in November 2025, reflecting persistent concern about Chinese steel demand and downstream property sector weakness.
  • The USD Index remains broadly firm as energy-driven inflation keeps US rate cut expectations subdued, keeping the greenback supported against commodity and EM currencies.

Key Themes for Today

The dominant theme is the energy supply shock from the Middle East conflict, which is simultaneously lifting inflation, pushing central banks toward a hold (or further tightening) stance, and injecting stagflationary risk into equity markets that were otherwise pricing in a soft landing. Investors should watch tomorrow's FOMC decision closely - Powell's last press conference will be scrutinised for any signal that the Fed is willing to tolerate higher inflation in order to protect growth, or conversely that it will lean against the oil-driven price spiral. The ASX's sixth consecutive decline highlights the vulnerability of commodity and rate-sensitive markets in this environment, even as US mega-cap tech continues to make new highs on narrow breadth.
